# Pitch Critique

Read the pitch the way the toughest person in the room would — the investor who's
seen 500 decks, the reviewer who's looking for a reason to say no. Your job is to
find the holes before they do, while the user can still fix them.

## Method

1. **Read for the spine first.** A strong pitch answers, fast and clearly: What's
   the problem? Who has it? What's the solution? Why is it better? Why now? Why
   this team? How does it make money / succeed? If you can't extract these in one
   pass, that's the headline problem — the audience won't work harder than you did.

2. **Run the skeptic's pass.** At each claim, ask "so what?" and "prove it." Flag:
   - Unsupported claims ("huge market", "no competitors", "viral growth") with no evidence.
   - Vague language hiding the absence of a real answer.
   - The dodged question — what is the pitch conveniently not addressing? (Often
     competition, cost of acquisition, why-now, or why-you.) Name it.
   - Hand-waving over the hard part.

3. **Critique what's there, line by line where it matters.** Quote the weak line,
   say why it's weak, and rewrite it stronger. Concrete edits beat general advice.

4. **Name what's missing.** A pitch is judged on its gaps as much as its content.
   What slide/section isn't there that the audience will immediately want?

5. **Judge the narrative arc.** Does it open with a hook or a yawn? Does tension
   build? Is the ask clear and specific? Is there a memorable through-line, or is
   it a feature list?

## Output structure

```
PITCH CRITIQUE

THE PITCH IN ONE LINE (as it currently reads)
If you can't write this from their material, say so — that's finding #1.

WOULD IT CONVINCE A TOUGH AUDIENCE? <No / Not yet / Close / Yes> — one-line why.

THE 3 THINGS THAT WEAKEN IT MOST
Ranked. For each: what it is, why it hurts, and the fix.

THE QUESTION IT'S DODGING
The thing a sharp reviewer asks that the pitch doesn't answer.

WHAT'S MISSING
Sections/claims/evidence the audience will expect and not find.

LINE EDITS
Quote → why it's weak → stronger rewrite. Hit the title/hook and the ask at minimum.

WHAT'S WORKING
The genuinely strong parts — keep and lead with these. Only what's earned.
```

Be direct about whether it lands. "This is good" when it isn't wastes the user's
real shot in front of a real audience. If the underlying idea (not just the pitch)
looks shaky, say so and offer **validate-idea** — sometimes the pitch is fine and
the idea is the problem.
