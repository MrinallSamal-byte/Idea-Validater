# Idea-Type Playbooks

Different kinds of ideas fail for different reasons. Identify the type, then apply
the matching lens. If an idea spans types (a startup with a hard technical core),
use both.

---

## Startup / business ideas

The graveyard is full of well-built products nobody wanted. Push hardest on
demand and distribution, not on the product.

Ask:
- Who is the customer, *specifically*, and how do you reach the first 10?
- Is this a painkiller or a vitamin? What do they use today, and why is that not fine?
- Why now? What changed (tech, regulation, behavior, cost) that makes this possible/needed today and not five years ago?
- What's the wedge — the narrow first beachhead — vs. the grand vision?
- Unit economics: roughly, does one customer make more than they cost to acquire and serve?
- Why won't an incumbent with distribution just add this as a feature?

Classic failure modes: no real demand, the market is too small, CAC exceeds LTV,
a well-funded incumbent crushes it, "we're like X but better" with no edge,
founder-market misfit.

---

## Product / feature ideas

The question is rarely "can we build it" — it's "should we, and for whom."

Ask:
- What user problem does this solve, and how do you know users have it (data, or a hunch)?
- What does this trade off against? Every feature adds complexity and cost — what's the price?
- Who is it *not* for? A feature for everyone usually delights no one.
- What's the cheapest way to test demand before building it (fake door, prototype, waitlist)?
- Does it deepen the core value or scatter focus?
- How will you know it worked — what metric moves, and by how much?

Classic failure modes: building for a loud minority, feature bloat, solving an
imagined problem, no success metric, cannibalizing something that already works.

---

## Technical / architecture ideas

Push on tradeoffs, failure modes, and whether the complexity is justified.

Ask:
- What problem does this solve that the simpler/boring approach doesn't? "Boring" is a strong default.
- What are you trading away (operational complexity, latency, cost, team familiarity, hiring pool)?
- How does it fail, and how do you detect and recover? What's the blast radius?
- What does this look like at 10x and 100x scale — does it hold, or are you building for traffic you don't have?
- Build vs. buy vs. adopt an existing tool — why build?
- Who maintains this in two years, and is the knowledge bus-factor > 1?

Classic failure modes: resume-driven / hype-driven architecture, premature
optimization, reinventing a mature tool, complexity with no payoff, a design that
only one person understands.

---

## Creative / content ideas

Judge on distinctiveness, audience, and sustainability — not just whether it's "cool."

Ask:
- Who is this for, and why would they choose it over the infinite alternatives competing for the same attention?
- What's the distinct angle or voice? "Like X but..." needs a real but.
- Can you sustain it? Most content ideas die from burnout, not bad concepts — what's the format/cadence you can actually keep up?
- Distribution: where does the audience already gather, and how does this reach them?
- Is there a reason it spreads or compounds, or does every piece start from zero?
- What does success look like — and is that outcome worth the effort to you?

Classic failure modes: no audience, undifferentiated, unsustainable cadence, no
distribution plan, mismatched to where the audience actually is, vanity over value.
